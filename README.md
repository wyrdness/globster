## Overview

A globster is an unidentified organic mass — usually large, pale or whitish, fibrous, and lacking obvious anatomy — that washes up on a beach or other shoreline. The term was coined by writer Ivan T. Sanderson in 1962 to describe a Tasmanian carcass discovered in 1960 that had 'no visible eyes, no defined head, and no apparent bone structure'. Globsters were widely cited in 20th-century cryptozoological literature as possible evidence of unknown sea creatures (including alleged 'sea monsters', surviving plesiosaurs, or giant octopuses). Since the late 1980s a series of histological and DNA studies — notably by Sidney K. Pierce and colleagues at the University of South Florida — has shown that almost every well-analyzed globster is the decomposed remains of a whale (or, in a few cases, a basking shark or other large vertebrate). The fibrous appearance is produced by the persistence of dense collagen fibres from blubber and connective tissue after softer tissues have decomposed. Notable cases include the St. Augustine Monster (Florida, 1896 — eventually identified as whale collagen), the Tasmanian Globster (1960), the Stronsay Beast (Orkney, 1808 — identified as a basking shark), the Bermuda Blobs (1988, 1997), the Newfoundland Blob (2001), and the Chilean Blob (2003). The category remains useful as a catch-all for shoreline carcasses pending identification.

## Distribution

Globsters have been reported on coastlines globally — Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Oceans, and adjacent seas. The phenomenon is not regional in any meaningful sense; it tracks the geography of large cetacean populations and shipping/observer density.

## Notable Sightings

### St. Augustine Monster ⭐
> **Date**: 1896-11-30  
> **Location**: Anastasia Island beach, near St. Augustine, Florida  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (Beachgoers (DeWitt Webb and others))  
> **Evidence**: Tissue samples preserved at the Smithsonian Institution; Photographs  
A massive (~5 tonne) pale, fibrous mass washed up on Anastasia Island. Yale zoologist Addison Emery Verrill initially proposed it was an enormous octopus, but later DNA and histology by Sidney K. Pierce and colleagues (1995, 2004) identified the tissue as whale collagen.
**Credibility**: high — Originally interpreted as a giant octopus; conclusively identified as whale by Pierce et al. (1995).
**Sources**: Verrill, A. E. (1897), American Journal of Science; Pierce, S. K. et al. (1995), Biological Bulletin

### Tasmanian Globster ⭐
> **Date**: 1960-08-01  
> **Location**: Western Tasmanian beach, near the Interview River mouth  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Cattle rancher (Ben Fenton))  
> **Evidence**: Tissue samples; Photographs; CSIRO investigation report  
A large white fibrous mass — about 6 m long, 3 m wide, ~5–10 tonnes — discovered on a remote beach. The Australian CSIRO investigated in 1962 and concluded it was decomposed whale or marine vertebrate tissue. Ivan T. Sanderson popularized the find and coined the term 'globster' in 1962.
**Credibility**: high — Origin of the term 'globster'.
**Sources**: Sanderson, I. T. (1962); CSIRO investigation report (1962)

### Chilean Blob ⭐
> **Date**: 2003-07-01  
> **Location**: Los Muermos beach, southern Chile  
> **Witnesses**: 0 (Local residents and Chilean Center for Cetacean Conservation researchers)

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Decomposed cetacean (whale) blubber** _(Sidney K. Pierce, Eugenie Clark, CSIRO marine biologists)_

Mainstream identification: globsters are the persisting collagen matrix of whale (especially sperm whale) blubber and connective tissue, after softer tissues have decomposed at sea.

**Basking shark and other large fish carcasses** _(Marine biologists, 19th–20th century)_

Some classic 'sea serpent' carcasses (notably the Stronsay Beast) are identified as decomposed basking sharks, whose vertebrae and supporting tissue can give a serpent-like silhouette in advanced decay.

### Skeptical

**No remaining cryptid hypothesis necessary** _(Daniel Loxton, Donald Prothero, Sidney K. Pierce)_

Given that every well-studied globster has resolved to known vertebrates, and that the fibrous appearance is fully explained by collagen persistence, mainstream science treats the cryptid 'sea-monster' framing as obsolete.
